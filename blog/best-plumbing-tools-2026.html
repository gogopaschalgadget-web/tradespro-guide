Best Plumbing Tools for Homeowners in 2026 — Complete Buyer's Guide

📅 March 25, 2026 📂 Plumbing ⏱️ 10 min read

Whether you're tackling a leaky faucet, unclogging a drain, or replacing a fixture, having the right plumbing tools can save you hundreds in service calls. This guide covers the 10 essential plumbing tools every homeowner should own—tools that professionals use daily and that pay for themselves after just one DIY project.

We've tested and researched each tool based on durability, ease of use, and real-world homeowner needs. These aren't gimmicks or bargain-bin tools; they're the workhorses that belong in every home toolbox.

1. Tongue-and-Groove Pliers (Channellock 440)

Tongue-and-groove pliers are the workhorse of plumbing. Unlike standard pliers, the sliding jaw lets you grip everything from small nuts to large pipes, adapting to whatever size you need. If you own only one plier, this should be it.

The Channellock 440 features a smooth sliding mechanism that never binds, serrated jaws that grip without slipping, and a comfortable grip even during extended use. It's the plier that plumbers reach for first because it works on virtually every fastener and fitting in your home.

You'll use these for tightening supply line nuts under sinks, adjusting coupling nuts, gripping large pipe fittings, and handling anything that needs a firm, adjustable grip. The 10-inch length gives you leverage without being unwieldy.

2. Pipe Wrench (RIDGID 14" Aluminum)

A pipe wrench is specifically designed for gripping cylindrical pipes. Unlike adjustable wrenches, the pipe wrench has a moving jaw that angles inward, locking onto pipes with tremendous grip force. If you ever need to remove or install a pipe, you need a pipe wrench.

The RIDGID 14" Aluminum pipe wrench is lightweight (reducing hand fatigue) while maintaining the strength to handle stubborn, corroded fittings. The smooth jaw opening mechanism never sticks, and the aluminum body won't rust or corrode, even in damp basements.

You'll use this for disconnecting old water lines, removing galvanized pipes, tightening threaded pipe connections, and any job where you need to grip a cylindrical object with serious force. The 14-inch length gives you leverage without being too heavy.

3. Basin Wrench (RIDGID 1017 Telescoping)

A basin wrench is the secret weapon for removing and installing faucets. It's specifically designed to fit into the tight space under sink basins where normal wrenches won't fit. If you've ever tried to loosen those nuts behind a sink without one, you know how frustrating that can be.

The RIDGID 1017 is a telescoping basin wrench, meaning it extends and retracts, fitting into even the most cramped under-sink spaces. The pivoting jaw grips the nut and rotates with a single motion, reaching bolts and nuts that would otherwise require removing the entire sink basin.

You'll use this for replacing or repairing kitchen and bathroom faucets. It typically saves 30 minutes of frustration per faucet replacement—time worth far more than the tool's cost. Even seasoned plumbers carry one because there's no alternative that works as well.

4. Plunger — Flange Style (Korky 99-4A)

Most homeowners think a plunger is a plunger, but there's a world of difference. A flange plunger (also called a toilet plunger) has an extra rubber extension that fits inside the toilet bowl, creating a watertight seal that standard cup plungers can't achieve. For toilet clogs, it's non-negotiable.

The Korky 99-4A is made from natural rubber that won't crack or harden over time, stays sealed to the toilet bowl for a full 100 plunges without losing suction, and features a comfort grip that prevents hand slipping. It's specifically engineered for toilet drain profiles.

You'll use this for clearing toilet bowl clogs caused by excess paper, debris, or minor blockages. Unlike chemical drain cleaners, a good plunger actually physically dislodges the blockage rather than trying to dissolve it. For 95% of household toilet clogs, a flange plunger solves the problem in under a minute.

5. Plumber's Tape / Teflon Tape (Oatey)

Teflon tape (also called plumber's tape or PTFE tape) is a thin white tape wrapped around male pipe threads to create a watertight seal. It's the simplest, cheapest, and most effective way to prevent leaks at threaded connections. Every plumber wraps every threaded connection—and you should too.

Oatey's plumber's tape is thicker than cheap alternatives, resists shredding during installation, and works on everything from hose connections to faucet supply lines to gas line connections. One roll lasts for hundreds of connections because you only use a few wraps per joint.

The application is simple: wrap the tape clockwise around the male threads 3–4 times, then screw the fitting together. The tape fills microscopic gaps in the threads, preventing water from seeping out. Without it, nearly every threaded connection will eventually weep or leak.

Pro Tip: Always wrap clockwise (looking at the fitting you're wrapping). The tape will shred if you go counterclockwise. Wrap 3–4 times for water connections, more for gas lines.

6. Tubing Cutter (RIDGID 101 Close Quarters)

The RIDGID 101 Close Quarters cutter is compact enough to fit in tight spaces (between joists, under cabinets) where larger cutters won't fit. It features a rolling cutter wheel that never dulls for years of use, and a built-in reamer that removes burrs automatically as you cut.

You'll use this for replacing damaged supply lines, shortening tubing to fit new configurations, or removing sections of old copper pipe. A clean, perpendicular cut is essential for proper fitting and leak prevention—something a hacksaw simply can't achieve reliably.

7. Drain Snake / Auger (RIDGID Power Spin+)

A drain snake (also called a plumbing auger) is a flexible cable that clears blockages deep in drain lines. Unlike plungers (which work via suction and force), snakes physically break up or retrieve the clog. For drains that plungers can't clear, a snake is your next move.

You'll use this for clearing hair clogs in bathroom drains, soap buildup in kitchen sinks, and blockages caused by roots or debris in floor drains. A motorized snake costs less than a single professional drain cleaning service and works on virtually every household drain.

9. Plumber's Putty (Oatey Stain-Free)

Plumber's putty is a moldable sealant used to create watertight seals around faucets, sink drains, and pop-up stoppers. Unlike caulk (which hardens), putty remains soft and moldable, allowing you to remove fixtures later without damage. It's the standard in plumbing for below-sink sealing.

Oatey's stain-free formula doesn't leave residue or stains on porcelain, chrome, or other fixtures—a critical advantage over generic putty. It stays pliable indefinitely, never hardens, and doesn't require drying time before use. One can handles dozens of installations.

You'll use this when installing new faucets or sink drains, sealing the connection between the fixture and the sink basin. Roll it into a rope, press it around the fixture base, tighten the fixture from below, and wipe away excess. It creates a permanent watertight seal that remains removable if you need to service the fixture later.

10. PEX Crimping Tool (SharkBite PEX Clamp Tool)

PEX tubing is increasingly common in modern homes because it's flexible, durable, and cheaper than copper. However, PEX requires proper crimping (compression) to create watertight connections. A quality crimping tool is essential for any PEX installation or repair work.

You'll use this for replacing PEX supply lines, extending PEX runs, or repairing damaged sections. PEX is becoming the standard in modern plumbing, so this tool future-proofs your ability to handle water line work in your home. One tool handles all PEX clamp sizes from 3/8" to 1".

Getting Started: The Starter Kit Approach

You don't need to buy all 10 tools at once. Start with the core four: tongue-and-groove pliers, flange plunger, adjustable wrench, and plumber's tape. These four tools handle 80% of household plumbing tasks and cost less than $60 total.

As you encounter specific projects, add specialized tools like the basin wrench (faucet work) or drain snake (stubborn clogs). Over time, you'll build a complete toolkit that handles virtually any household plumbing challenge.

Pro Tip: Keep your plumbing tools in a dedicated drawer or toolbag. When an emergency hits (leaky valve, running toilet), you'll appreciate having everything in one place and ready to go.

Common Plumbing Tasks You Can Now Handle

  • Leaky Faucets: Basin wrench + plumber's tape + plumber's putty
  • Running Toilets: Plunger (if it's a clog), or shutoff valve with adjustable wrench to isolate the problem
  • Dripping Pipes: Tongue-and-groove pliers to tighten leaking connections
  • Drain Clogs: Plunger first, then drain snake if needed
  • Supply Line Replacement: Pipe wrench, tubing cutter, and clamps (if PEX)
  • Fixture Installation: Basin wrench, adjustable wrench, plumber's tape, plumber's putty

With these tools and the knowledge of how to use them, you'll save thousands in service calls over your lifetime as a homeowner.
