General Contractor Bid Template: Win More Projects

Published April 6, 2026 | 10 min read

A bad bid kills your business in three ways. First, you lose the job because your bid is unclear or doesn't match the customer's expectations. Second, you win the job but lose money because you underestimated scope. Third, you create confusion about what work is included, leading to scope creep and customer disputes.

A great bid is crystal clear about what you're building, what it costs, and what it doesn't include. This guide shows you the exact structure top contractors use to close projects faster and avoid low-margin nightmares.

The 5 Critical Elements of a Winning Bid

Every professional bid should have these five elements. If you're missing one, you're losing jobs or money.

1. Professional Header and Job Information

Start with your company name, logo, address, phone, email, and license number. Include the customer's name, address, and contact information. Add a bid date and bid expiration date (usually 30 days). If the customer doesn't accept your bid within 30 days, materials costs may have changed, so your bid is no longer valid.

2. Detailed Scope of Work

List every task you're doing, in order, with enough detail that a third party could read it and understand exactly what work you're performing. "Bathroom remodel" is vague. "Bathroom remodel including demolition of existing fixtures, new 3 x 5 tile shower with 6 x 6 subway tile walls and 12 x 12 floor tile, new toilet, new vanity with cultured marble sink, new faucet, paint walls and ceiling, install new lighting, and new exhaust fan" is clear.

3. Itemized Pricing

Break down costs by major sections (materials, labor, subcontractors, permits, cleanup). This allows customers to see exactly where their money is going and understand the value of different portions of the work.

4. What's NOT Included

This is the section that saves you from scope creep and disputes. Explicitly list what's outside the bid scope. Examples: "Bid does NOT include: moving existing electrical outlets, additional drywall repair beyond the bathroom, crown molding, or any wall modifications."

5. Terms and Conditions

Include payment schedule (deposit, progress payments, final payment), timeline, warranty, and terms for change orders. Example: "50% deposit required to reserve the job, 50% due upon completion. Timeline: 2-3 weeks pending permit approval. All work covered by 1-year workmanship warranty."

DEMOLITION - Remove existing toilet, vanity, mirror, and fixtures - Remove existing flooring and wall tile - Demo grout and backer board - Haul away debris (included) 2. ELECTRICAL - Run new electrical line for vanity lighting - Install GFCI outlet for vanity - Install exhaust fan ductwork and wiring 4. CARPENTRY - Install cement backer board for shower - Install new vanity cabinet and substrate - Frame for mirror and shelving 5. TILE AND FINISHES - Install 6x6 subway tile on shower walls (3x5 shower) - Install 12x12 porcelain floor tile - Install new grout and sealant - Paint walls and ceiling (semi-gloss) 6. FIXTURES AND HARDWARE - New 2-handle faucet (Chrome, mid-range) - New toilet (Standard white, 2-piece) - New exhaust fan with damper - New vanity mirror (24x36) - Towel bar and TP holder 7. FINAL CLEANUP - Remove all job site debris - Clean bathroom thoroughly - Protective coverings removed ================================================== PRICING ================================================== Materials & Supplies: $3,800 Labor (80 hours @ $75/hr): $6,000 Subcontractors (Plumbing): $1,500 Permits & Inspections: $ 400 Waste Removal: $ 200 Sales Tax (8.5%): $ 870 ========= TOTAL BID: $12,770 ================================================== WHAT IS NOT INCLUDED ================================================== This bid does NOT include: - Moving electrical outlets beyond the described locations - Removal of tile from walls or floor beyond bathroom (does not include hall) - Crown molding or decorative trim - Heated towel racks or luxury fixtures - Changes to door or doorway framing - Asbestos abatement if discovered - Structural repairs if water damage is found during demolition - Any tile pattern changes after approval ================================================== TERMS AND CONDITIONS ================================================== PAYMENT SCHEDULE: - 50% ($6,385) due to reserve job date (upon acceptance) - 50% ($6,385) due upon final completion and inspection TIMELINE: - Work begins: 2 weeks from deposit (pending permit approval) - Expected completion: 14 work days - Warranty: 1 year on workmanship, 5 years on materials CHANGE ORDERS: Any changes to scope must be approved in writing. Additional work will be billed at $75/hour labor plus materials at cost plus 30% markup. PERMITS: Customer is responsible for all inspections. Contractor will apply for permits if requested (additional $100-200 fee).

This bid is clear, detailed, and protects both parties. The customer knows exactly what they're getting. You know exactly what you're doing. The "What's Not Included" section prevents scope creep arguments.

How to Write Your Own Bid: Step-by-Step

Step 1: Visit the Site and Scope the Work (1-2 hours)

Walk through the project. Take photos. Measure everything. Ask the customer clarifying questions: "Do you want to reuse any existing fixtures? What's your timeline? What's your budget range?" Write down every detail that affects cost.

Step 2: Calculate Materials (30 minutes)

Get pricing from your suppliers. Request written quotes if costs are significant. Add 5-10% for waste and contingency on material quantities.

Step 3: Estimate Labor (30 minutes)

Based on the scope, estimate hours for each task. If you're unsure, estimate conservatively (more hours, not fewer). Example: "bathroom demo 4 hours, plumbing 16 hours, electrical 8 hours, tile 20 hours, finishing 12 hours = 60 total hours." Multiply by your hourly rate ($50-100 depending on your market).

Step 4: Add Subcontractor and Overhead Costs (15 minutes)

If you're hiring plumber, electrician, or specialist tile person, get their quotes. Add overhead for permits, inspections, cleanup, equipment rental, and contingency (10-15% of total costs).

Step 5: Write the Detailed Scope (30 minutes)

Be specific. Not "tile bathroom" but "install 12x12 porcelain floor tile in bathroom only, grout with unsanded grout, seal grout per manufacturer specs." The more detail, the fewer surprises.

Step 6: Create "What's Not Included" Section (15 minutes)

Think about every way a customer might misinterpret your scope. List everything you're NOT doing. This prevents misunderstandings that turn into disputes.

Step 7: Set Terms and Conditions (10 minutes)

Payment schedule, timeline, warranty, change order process. Keep it simple. Use standard language.

Common Bid Mistakes to Avoid

  • Vague scope: "Remodel bathroom" is too vague. Say exactly what fixtures, materials, and finishes you're installing.
  • Underestimating labor: "Bathroom tile 6 hours" is likely a guess. Be honest about time. If you're not sure, ask an experienced contractor or add 20% buffer.
  • Forgetting overhead: Material plus labor is not your bid price. Add permits, inspections, waste removal, equipment, and contingency.
  • No "what's not included": This creates disputes. Always have a section clarifying what's outside the scope.
  • Long timeline without penalty clause: If you say "2-3 months" and then take 4 months, customers get frustrated. Put a date and add "Delays due to customer decisions will extend the timeline proportionally."
  • No change order process: When a customer wants extra work mid-project, have a clear process (written change order, approval, additional cost).

Bid Presentation Tips

Print it professionally

Use quality paper, color if possible, and include your logo. A professional bid is more likely to be accepted than an email PDF.

Present it in person when possible

When you present the bid face-to-face, you can explain the scope, answer questions, and build confidence. Customers are more likely to hire you if they've talked to you about the project.

Follow up within 3 days

If you don't hear back within 3 days, follow up: "Hi Sarah, I wanted to check in and see if you have any questions about the bathroom remodel bid. Happy to discuss any changes or options. Let me know what works best."

Compare to competitor bids

If you lose a bid, ask why: "I understand you chose another contractor. If you're comfortable sharing, I'd love to know if it was pricing, timeline, or something else. This helps me improve." Don't bad-mouth competitors, just understand the objection.

Bid Checklist (Use Before Submitting Any Bid):
  • Professional header with company info and license number
  • Customer name, address, contact, and bid date
  • Detailed scope of work (specific materials, quantities, finishes)
  • Itemized pricing (materials, labor, subs, permits, overhead)
  • Clear "What's Not Included" section
  • Payment schedule (when money is due)
  • Timeline and warranty
  • Change order process explained
  • Professional formatting and spelling check
  • Bid expiration date (30 days is standard)

Why Detailed Bids Win More Jobs

Customers get 2-3 bids per project. Most bids are generic and unclear. A detailed, professional bid that shows you've thought through every aspect of the work builds confidence. It says, "This contractor knows what they're doing, has done this before, and has thought about edge cases."

A clear bid also eliminates disputes. When scope is crystal clear and "what's not included" is listed, customers can't claim they expected extra work. This protects your profit margin and your reputation.
