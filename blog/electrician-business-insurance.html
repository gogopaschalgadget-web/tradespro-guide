Electrician Business Insurance: What You Actually Need

Published April 6, 2026 | 9 min read

Electrical work is high-risk. A miswired outlet can cause a house fire. A poorly installed panel can harm someone. This is why electricians are frequently sued and why insurance is non-negotiable. The question isn't whether to carry insurance, it's what type and how much.

This guide covers the five types of insurance every electrician should consider, what each costs in 2026, and how to choose coverage that protects your business without over-insuring.

The Five Essential Insurance Types for Electricians

Insurance Type What It Covers Cost (Annual) Must-Have?
General Liability Property damage, bodily injury, legal defense $600-2000 Yes
Tools and Equipment Theft, loss, or damage to your tools $300-800 Yes
Workers Compensation Employee injuries, medical costs, lost wages $2000-8000+ Required if you hire employees
Professional Liability (E&O) Design errors, missed code violations, negligence claims $400-1200 Recommended

1. General Liability Insurance

This is the foundation of your business coverage. General liability covers property damage and bodily injury caused by your work. If you accidentally damage a customer's kitchen when running cable, general liability covers the repair costs and legal defense.

What General Liability Covers

  • Property damage to a customer's home (fire, structural damage, broken fixtures)
  • Bodily injury (someone hurt on site due to your work)
  • Legal defense costs if sued
  • Medical bills and lost wages for injured parties
  • Advertising injury (slander, copyright infringement in marketing)

Coverage limits you should carry:

  • Per-incident limit: $1 million (covers one event)
  • Annual aggregate limit: $2 million (covers all events in a year)
  • Deductible: $500-1000 (lowers your premium if you accept higher risk)

Cost: $600-2000 annually depending on your revenue, claims history, and location. New electricians typically pay $800-1200. Experienced electricians with no claims pay $600-900.

Real-world example: You drill through an electrical wire hidden in a wall. The wire shorts, causes a small fire, and damages $8000 worth of drywall and finish work. Your general liability pays the damage plus legal fees.

2. Tools and Equipment Insurance

Your tools are your income. A stolen $5000 power tool can shut your business down for a week. Tools and equipment insurance covers theft, loss, and damage.

What Tools and Equipment Insurance Covers

  • Theft from your van or job site
  • Accidental damage (dropping tools, weather damage)
  • Loss due to fire or flood
  • Usually covers tools in your vehicle 24/7

What it typically does NOT cover:

  • Tools left at a customer's home or job site unsecured
  • Tools damaged due to normal wear and tear
  • Loss from leaving your van unlocked for extended periods

Cost: $300-800 annually. You usually pay a percentage of your tool inventory value (0.8-2% of total tool value). If you have $10000 in tools, expect $80-200 annually.

Tip: Maintain a list of your tools with serial numbers and photos. This speeds up claims processing.

3. Workers Compensation Insurance

Once you hire your first employee, workers compensation is mandatory in almost every state. If an employee is injured on the job, workers comp covers medical bills, lost wages, and rehabilitation costs.

State requirement: Check with your state's Workers Compensation Board or department of labor. You can't legally operate with employees without workers comp.

Cost: $2000-8000+ annually depending on your payroll and state. The insurance carrier calculates your rate based on payroll size and claims history. A typical rate for electrical work is 8-15% of total payroll.

Example: You hire a helper earning $25/hour, working 40 hours per week for 50 weeks per year. Annual payroll is $50000. At a 10% rate, workers comp costs $5000 annually.

Critical: Do not skip workers compensation if you hire employees. A single serious injury without coverage can cost $100000+ in medical bills and lost wages. Additionally, operating without it is a criminal violation in most states.

4. Professional Liability Insurance (Errors and Omissions)

This covers claims that your work was negligent, late, or didn't meet code. If you run a circuit wrong and it doesn't pass inspection, or you miss a required grounding requirement, professional liability covers legal defense and damages.

What Professional Liability Covers

  • Design errors or improper installation
  • Failure to pull permits or pass inspection
  • Code violations discovered after job completion
  • Customer claims of negligent work

Cost: $400-1200 annually. Many insurers bundle professional liability with general liability at a discount.

Difference from general liability: General liability covers accidents during work (you damage something). Professional liability covers mistakes in the work itself (you did the work wrong).

Real-world example: You miss a code requirement in a panel install. Inspector fails it. Customer sues you for the cost of bringing it up to code plus time lost. Professional liability covers the settlement and legal defense.

5. Commercial Auto Insurance

Your personal auto insurance will NOT cover business use of your vehicle. If you're in an accident while traveling to a job, your personal policy can deny the claim. Commercial auto covers you while working.

Coverage you need:

  • Liability: $100000-300000 per person, $500000-1000000 per accident
  • Collision: covers damage to your vehicle in an accident
  • Comprehensive: covers theft, weather, vandalism
  • Uninsured/underinsured motorist: covers you if hit by someone without insurance

Cost: $800-2000 annually. Price varies based on your driving record, vehicle type, and claims history.

Insurance Cost Summary (New Solo Electrician)

  • General Liability: $900
  • Tools and Equipment: $500
  • Commercial Auto: $1200
  • Professional Liability: $600
  • Total: $3200 per year

This breaks down to $267 per month or roughly 3-5% of total revenue for a solo electrician billing $60000-80000 annually.

Money-Saving Tips

Bundle Your Policies

Most insurance companies offer a 10-20% discount if you buy multiple policies from them. Bundling general liability, professional liability, and tools insurance together saves $400-600 annually.

Increase Your Deductible

Choosing a $1000 deductible instead of $500 lowers your premium by 15-25%. Only do this if you have cash reserves to cover a deductible if you need to file a claim.

Pay Annual, Not Monthly

Paying your annual premium in one lump sum instead of monthly installments saves 10-15% on finance fees.

Maintain a Clean Claims History

The best way to keep insurance costs low is to not file claims. After 2-3 years with no claims, your rate drops significantly. Safe work practices save money.

Finding Insurance Providers

Get quotes from at least 3 providers:
  1. Local independent insurance agents (often best for trades)
  2. National carriers (State Farm, GEICO, Progressive)
  3. Specialty trade insurance companies (ContractorCare, Builders Risk, Hiscox)

Ask for quotes on the exact coverage you need. Compare not just price, but deductibles, coverage limits, and customer reviews on claims handling.

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Operating without general liability. This is the most common cause of electrician business failure.
  • Workers comp to friends or family as subcontractors to avoid paying workers comp. This is illegal and can result in fines and criminal charges.
  • Underinsuring to save money. A single lawsuit from a serious injury can exceed your annual insurance cost by 50-100x.
  • Not informing your insurance about major changes in your business (hiring employees, moving to a new state, change in specialization).

Insurance Review Checklist

  • Get general liability with $1M per-incident limit
  • Add tools and equipment coverage
  • Add commercial auto if you use a work vehicle
  • Add professional liability for design or code work
  • If hiring: get workers compensation BEFORE the first employee starts
  • Review your policy annually for coverage gaps
  • Maintain documentation of your business operations to support premium negotiations
