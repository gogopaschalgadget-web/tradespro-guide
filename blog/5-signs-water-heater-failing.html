5 Signs Your Water Heater Is About to Fail

March 23, 2026 · Plumbing · 6 min read

Your water heater works quietly in the background every day, providing hot water for showers, dishes, and laundry. Most homeowners don't think about it until something goes wrong — and by then, the damage is done. The average water heater replacement costs between $1,500 and $3,500, but catching problems early can save you from emergency bills and water damage.

Here are the five most reliable warning signs that your water heater is reaching the end of its life.

1. Age Over 8-12 Years

The single most important factor is age. Tank-style water heaters typically last 8 to 12 years, while tankless models can last 15 to 20. Check the serial number on your unit — the first two digits often represent the year of manufacture. If your tank water heater is over 10 years old, it's time to start planning a replacement even if it still works.

Pro Tip: Write the installation date on a sticker and attach it to the unit. Future you (or the next homeowner) will thank you.

2. Rusty or Discolored Hot Water

If your hot water comes out with a reddish or brown tint — but the cold water runs clear — the inside of your tank is likely corroding. The sacrificial anode rod, which protects the tank from rust, has been consumed. Once the tank itself starts corroding, leaks are just a matter of time.

To confirm the issue, drain a few gallons from the tank into a bucket. If the water is rusty after the first 5 gallons, the tank is the source. If it clears up quickly, the discoloration may be from your pipes instead.

3. Strange Noises — Rumbling, Popping, or Banging

As sediment builds up on the bottom of the tank, it hardens and creates a layer of insulation between the burner and the water. This forces the heater to work harder and produces rumbling or popping sounds as water pushes through the sediment layer. The extra effort accelerates wear, reduces efficiency, and can eventually cause the tank to crack.

Warning: If you hear loud banging or knocking, turn off the heater and call a professional immediately. This could indicate dangerous pressure buildup.

4. Water Pooling Around the Base

Even small amounts of moisture around the base of your water heater deserve attention. As the metal tank heats and cools thousands of times, micro-fractures can develop. These may only leak when the tank is hot and the metal expands, which is why the puddle seems to appear and disappear.

Before assuming the worst, check the temperature and pressure relief valve (T&P valve) and the inlet/outlet connections. Tighten any loose fittings. If the leak is coming from the tank body itself, replacement is the only option — tank leaks cannot be repaired.

5. Inconsistent or Declining Hot Water

If your showers are getting shorter, your water temperature fluctuates unexpectedly, or it takes much longer to reheat, the heating element or thermostat may be failing. In gas water heaters, sediment buildup or a failing thermocouple can cause similar problems. In electric models, one or both heating elements may have burned out.

A single element failure in an electric heater can sometimes be repaired for $150-$300. But if both elements are failing in an older unit, the cost of repair approaches that of a new, more efficient model.

What to Do Next

If you've spotted one or more of these signs, don't wait for a catastrophic failure. A planned replacement on your schedule is always cheaper and less stressful than an emergency one. Get quotes from 2-3 licensed plumbers, consider upgrading to a high-efficiency or tankless model, and factor in any available utility rebates.
