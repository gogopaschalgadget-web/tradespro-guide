How Much Does Electrical Work Cost in 2026? Complete Price Guide

March 25, 2026 · Electrical · 8 min read

Whether you need a new outlet installed, your electrical panel upgraded, or an entire house rewired, knowing what electrical work costs before you call an electrician is the single best way to avoid overpaying. Electrical work pricing varies dramatically depending on the job, your location, the age of your home, and whether permits are required — and most homeowners have no idea what a fair price looks like until they are already holding a quote.

This guide breaks down the real cost of electrical work in 2026, covering 15 of the most common residential electrical jobs. Every price range is based on national averages adjusted for current labor rates and material costs. Use these numbers as your baseline when comparing quotes from local electricians.

How Electricians Charge: Hourly vs. Flat Rate

Before diving into specific job costs, it helps to understand how electricians typically price their work. Most residential electricians use one of two models, and the one they choose affects your total bill significantly.

Hourly rate: The national average electrician hourly rate in 2026 is $75 to $150 per hour, with master electricians and those in high-cost-of-living areas charging $150 to $200+. Apprentices and journeymen typically bill at the lower end. Hourly billing is more common for troubleshooting, diagnostic work, and smaller repairs where the scope is unpredictable.

Flat rate (per-job pricing): For well-defined jobs like outlet installations, panel upgrades, and ceiling fan installations, most electricians quote a flat price that includes labor and materials. Flat rate tends to favor the homeowner because it eliminates surprise overages — the price is the price, even if the job takes longer than expected.

Pro tip: Always ask for a flat-rate quote when the job scope is clear. If the electrician insists on hourly billing for a straightforward job like installing an outlet, that is a yellow flag. On top of the electrician's labor, expect to pay a service call fee of $50 to $100 just for showing up. Some electricians waive this if you hire them for the job. Always ask upfront whether the quote includes the service call or if it is added on top.

Outlet and Switch Installation Costs

Outlet and switch work is the bread and butter of residential electrical service calls. A standard 120V outlet installation runs $150 to $300 and takes an experienced electrician 30 to 60 minutes. The outlet itself costs $3 to $10 — you are paying almost entirely for labor, expertise, and the guarantee that it is wired safely to code.

GFCI outlets — the ones with the test/reset buttons required in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, and outdoor areas — cost slightly more at $200 to $400 per outlet. The GFCI device itself is $15 to $25, but installation may require running new wiring if your existing circuit does not have a ground wire. Homes built before 1970 commonly have this issue, which can add $100 to $200 per outlet in additional labor.

240V outlets for electric dryers, ranges, or EV chargers cost $300 to $800 because they require heavier gauge wiring (typically 10 AWG or 6 AWG) and a dedicated circuit breaker. If the outlet location is far from your panel or requires routing wire through finished walls, expect to pay toward the higher end of that range.

Safety warning: Never attempt to install a 240V outlet yourself unless you are a licensed electrician. Unlike standard 120V outlets, mistakes with 240V circuits can cause immediate electrocution, house fires, and will almost certainly fail inspection. This is one area where the cost of professional installation is non-negotiable.

Replacing a standard light switch is one of the cheapest electrical jobs at $100 to $250. Upgrading to a smart dimmer switch costs slightly more ($150 to $350) because smart switches often require a neutral wire that older homes may not have in the switch box. Ask your electrician to check for a neutral wire before purchasing an expensive smart switch — if your home lacks one, you will need a switch specifically rated for no-neutral installations, like the Lutron Caseta line.

Electrical Panel Upgrade Cost in 2026

If your home still has a 100-amp electrical panel — or worse, a fuse box — upgrading to a 200-amp panel is one of the most important electrical investments you can make. Modern homes with central air conditioning, electric dryers, EV chargers, and home offices routinely pull more power than a 100-amp panel can safely deliver. The result is tripped breakers, flickering lights, and in the worst case, overheated wiring behind your walls.

A 200-amp panel upgrade costs $1,800 to $4,500 nationally, with the wide range driven by three factors: whether your utility company needs to upgrade the service entrance (the connection from the power line to your home), whether your local jurisdiction requires a full inspection of existing wiring, and the physical difficulty of the installation in your home.

Here is what you can expect the cost to break down into:

If your electrician recommends a service entrance upgrade, get this confirmed with your utility company before agreeing. Some utilities perform their portion of the upgrade at no charge, while others bill $500 to $1,500. Your electrician should coordinate this — if they tell you to "call the utility yourself," consider that a sign of inexperience with panel upgrades.

EV Charger Installation Cost

With EV adoption accelerating in 2026, Level 2 home charger installation has become one of the fastest-growing electrical jobs. A Level 2 charger (240V, 40-50 amps) delivers 25 to 30 miles of range per hour of charging — compared to 4 to 5 miles per hour on a standard wall outlet. For most EV owners, this is the difference between waking up to a full battery and spending your afternoon at a public charger.

The total cost for EV charger installation ranges from $800 to $2,500, depending on three key variables:

1. Distance from the panel to the charger location. Every foot of 6-gauge wire between your panel and the garage or driveway adds cost. If the run is under 30 feet, you are looking at $800 to $1,200 total. Over 50 feet pushes toward $1,500 to $2,000+.

2. Panel capacity. If your panel is already at 80%+ capacity, you may need a panel upgrade ($1,800 to $4,500 additional) or a smart load management device ($200 to $500) that shares capacity between your EV charger and other large appliances. A qualified electrician will perform a load calculation before recommending either option.

3. The charger itself. Hardwired chargers like the Tesla Wall Connector ($400 to $500) and the ChargePoint Home Flex ($600 to $700) are the most popular options. Plug-in NEMA 14-50 chargers are cheaper but slightly less efficient and take up an outlet.

Tax credit alert: The federal EV charger tax credit (Section 30C) covers 30% of installation costs up to $1,000 for residential installations through 2032. This can reduce your effective cost by $300 to $750. Check with your tax advisor for eligibility — the property must be in an eligible census tract. Many states and utilities offer additional rebates on top of the federal credit.

Whole-House Rewiring: When You Need It and What It Costs

Rewiring an entire house is the most expensive and disruptive electrical project a homeowner will face, but for homes with outdated wiring, it is also the most important one from a safety perspective. Homes built before 1965 may have knob-and-tube wiring. Homes from the 1960s and 1970s may have aluminum branch wiring. Both are associated with significantly higher fire risk according to the National Fire Protection Association.

A whole-house rewire for a typical 1,500-square-foot home costs $8,000 to $20,000, with the primary cost driver being how accessible your walls and ceilings are. Here is the general breakdown by house type:

A rewire project includes replacing all branch circuit wiring, installing a new 200-amp panel, adding GFCI and AFCI protection where code requires it, and bringing the number of circuits up to modern standards. Most homes built before 1980 have far too few circuits for today's electrical demands — a modern kitchen alone requires 7 dedicated circuits (two small appliance, refrigerator, dishwasher, garbage disposal, microwave, and lighting).

If a full rewire is beyond your budget, ask your electrician about a partial rewire that prioritizes the highest-risk circuits: kitchen, bathrooms, bedrooms (where AFCI protection prevents arc-fault fires), and any circuits on the original knob-and-tube or aluminum wiring. A partial rewire targeting these critical areas typically costs $3,000 to $6,000 and addresses 80% of the fire risk.

How to Save Money on Electrical Work

Electrical work is one area where you should never cut corners on safety, but there are legitimate ways to reduce costs without compromising quality.

Bundle multiple jobs into one visit. That $50 to $100 service call fee applies per visit. If you need three outlets replaced, a ceiling fan installed, and a dimmer switch added, schedule them all at once. Most electricians will discount the per-item rate for multi-job visits because they eliminate repeat drive time and setup.

Buy your own fixtures and devices. Electricians typically mark up materials 15% to 30%. Buying your own GFCI outlets, ceiling fans, and light fixtures yourself eliminates this markup. Just confirm with the electrician beforehand that they will install homeowner-supplied materials — some will, some won't.

Get three quotes minimum. Electrical pricing varies more than almost any other trade because licensing requirements and market competition differ dramatically by region. In our analysis, the spread between the lowest and highest quote for the same job averages 40% to 60%. Three quotes takes an hour of phone calls and can save you hundreds.

Ask about off-season discounts. Electricians are busiest in spring and summer when construction is booming and homeowners are tackling projects. Scheduling non-urgent work for November through February often yields 10% to 20% lower rates simply because the electrician has more availability.

Check for utility rebates. Many electric utilities offer rebates for panel upgrades, EV charger installations, smart thermostats, and whole-house surge protectors. These rebates range from $50 to $500 and are often stackable with manufacturer rebates. Your electrician may or may not know about these — check your utility's website directly under "rebates" or "incentives." Red Flags When Hiring an Electrician

Electrical work is safety-critical, and hiring the wrong electrician can create hazards that remain hidden inside your walls for years. Here are the warning signs that should make you walk away from a contractor:

No license or insurance. Every state requires electricians to hold a license. Ask for the license number and verify it with your state's licensing board online. If they hesitate, move on. Also confirm they carry general liability insurance and workers' compensation — if an unlicensed, uninsured electrician is injured in your home, you may be liable.

No permit for permitted work. Panel upgrades, new circuit installations, and rewiring all require permits in every jurisdiction. An electrician who says "we don't need to pull a permit" is either cutting corners or unlicensed. Unpermitted electrical work can void your homeowner's insurance, create problems when selling your home, and leaves you with no recourse if the work is substandard.

Quoting without seeing the job. Any electrician who gives you a firm price over the phone for anything beyond a simple outlet replacement is guessing. A professional electrician will want to see the panel, assess the existing wiring, check for access issues, and understand the full scope before committing to a price. Phone quotes are estimates at best.

Pressure to decide immediately. "This price is only good today" is a high-pressure sales tactic. Reputable electricians give you a written quote that is valid for 30 days. If they are pressuring you to commit on the spot, they are more concerned with closing the sale than doing quality work.

What Affects Electrical Work Cost the Most?

Five factors account for most of the price variation you will see between quotes and between regions:

1. Geographic location. Electricians in San Francisco, New York, and Boston charge $125 to $200+ per hour. In rural areas and lower-cost-of-living states like Alabama, Arkansas, and Mississippi, rates drop to $60 to $90 per hour. This single factor can cause the same job to cost twice as much in one city versus another.

2. Age of your home. Homes built before 1970 almost always cost more to work on. Older wiring methods, lack of grounding, outdated panels (especially Federal Pacific and Zinsco, both known fire hazards), and plaster walls that are harder to fish wire through all increase labor time by 25% to 50%.

3. Accessibility. Running new wire through an unfinished basement or attic is fast and cheap. Running the same wire through finished walls, across a slab foundation with no crawlspace, or through a multi-story home with no attic access can triple the labor time. If your electrician quotes significantly more than the averages in this guide, accessibility is usually the reason.

4. Permit requirements. Permits add $100 to $400 to the job cost, plus the time for the electrician to schedule and attend the inspection. While some homeowners see this as an unnecessary expense, the inspection is your only guarantee that the work meets current safety codes. Always insist on permitted work for panel upgrades, new circuits, and rewiring.

5. Emergency vs. scheduled work. Emergency electrical service (weekends, holidays, after-hours) typically costs 1.5x to 2x the standard rate. If your issue is not an immediate safety hazard — no burning smell, no sparking, no exposed wires — schedule the repair during normal business hours and save 30% to 50%.

Bottom Line: Know the Numbers Before You Call

Electrical work is not an area where you want to be surprised by the bill. The difference between a fair price and an inflated one is simply knowing what the job should cost before the electrician arrives. Use the price ranges in this guide as your benchmark, get at least three quotes for any job over $500, always verify licensing and insurance, and never skip the permit for major work.

For smaller projects, investing in quality electrical testing tools and learning basic diagnostics (like using a non-contact voltage tester to identify dead circuits) can save you multiple service calls per year. Just know your limits — anything inside the panel, anything 240V, and anything involving new circuit wiring belongs to a licensed professional.
