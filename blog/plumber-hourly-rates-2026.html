How Much Does a Plumber Charge Per Hour in 2026? Complete Rate Guide

March 25, 2026 · Plumbing · 8 min read

If you're facing a plumbing issue, the first question isn't always "can I fix this?"—it's "how much will this cost?" Understanding plumber hourly rates helps you budget accurately, know when to call, and avoid surprise bills. We've compiled 2026 pricing data across regions, service types, and experience levels to give you the complete picture.

National Average Plumber Rates in 2026

The national average for plumber hourly rates ranges from $75 to $150 per hour for standard daytime service calls. However, this varies significantly based on whether it's an emergency, your location, the plumber's experience level, and local market conditions.

Regional Rate Breakdown

Plumbing costs vary dramatically by region. Metropolitan areas and high cost-of-living states naturally command premium rates, while rural areas typically offer lower pricing.

Northeast (NY, MA, CT, NJ)

$110–$200/hour

Premium rates due to high cost of living and strong union presence. Major cities like Boston and New York average $150–$200.

California & West Coast

$100–$180/hour

San Francisco and Los Angeles exceed $180. Smaller CA cities run $100–$130. High demand and licensing standards support higher rates.

Midwest (IL, OH, MI, MN)

$85–$140/hour

Most affordable region outside the South. Chicago commands $120–$150, while rural areas run $70–$100.

South (TX, FL, GA, NC)

$80–$130/hour

Generally competitive rates. Houston and Miami average $110–$140. Smaller towns offer $70–$100, making it the most budget-friendly region.

Mountain West (CO, AZ, UT)

$90–$150/hour

Denver tops the region at $130–$150. Lower population areas average $80–$110. Seasonal demand (winter) can spike rates 15–20%.

Pacific Northwest (WA, OR)

$95–$160/hour

Seattle and Portland range $140–$160. Licensed plumbers with union backing command premium pricing. Smaller cities run $85–$110.

What's Included in Hourly Rates?

Not all $100/hour rates are created equal. Here's what you're actually paying for:

Standard Hourly Rate Includes:

  • Labor (the plumber's time on-site)
  • Basic service call (diagnosis and assessment)
  • Travel time (sometimes)
  • Standard tools and equipment
  • Overhead (insurance, licensing, vehicle maintenance)

Usually NOT Included (Extra Costs):

  • Materials/parts — marked up 15–50% above wholesale
  • Permits — $50–$500+ depending on local jurisdiction
  • Specialized equipment rentals — drain cameras, pressure testing, etc.
  • Hazmat disposal — for old pipes (lead, asbestos)
  • Emergency surcharge — 25–100% markup for nights/weekends/holidays
Watch Out: Some plumbers quote hourly rates but charge a 2-hour minimum, even for 30-minute jobs. Always clarify minimums and whether the diagnostic fee applies if you don't hire them.

Flat Rate vs. Hourly: Which Saves Money?

Smart plumbers now offer flat rates for common jobs instead of hourly billing. This protects you from overbilling and gives the plumber incentive to work efficiently.

Flat-rate examples for common jobs:

  • Running toilet repair: $150–$250 flat
  • Faucet replacement: $200–$400 flat (including parts)
  • Garbage disposal swap: $250–$500 flat
  • Water heater replacement: $1,500–$3,500 flat (varying by unit type)
  • Main water line replacement: $2,000–$8,000 flat (highly variable)

For unique or complex problems (like a slab leak or severe main line damage), most plumbers will quote hourly with an estimate of expected duration.

How to Avoid Overpaying

Best Practices:
  • Get 2–3 quotes from licensed plumbers before hiring
  • Ask if the diagnostic fee is waived if you hire them
  • Request a written estimate with labor + parts breakdown
  • Clarify any surcharges (service call, travel, after-hours)
  • Ask about flat-rate pricing for your specific job
  • Check reviews and verify licensing through your state

Is Your Plumbing Bill Fair?

A $500 invoice for a 3-hour job at $100/hour + $200 in parts is reasonable. A $500 bill for 45 minutes of work is likely overcharging. Use this guide to assess whether your local rates align with the 2026 national benchmarks. If a quote seems unusually high, get a second opinion.
